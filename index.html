Tips for beginners

No shortcuts — just the habits every great player built their game on first.

Dribble with both hands

Spend equal time on your weak hand. A player who can only handle the ball with one hand is easy to guard — practice figure-eights and crossovers until both feel natural.

Shoot close, then step back

Build your form a few feet from the hoop before moving out. A repeatable, balanced shot from close range will hold up much better once you extend to the three-point line.

Learn to pivot

Once you pick up your dribble, one foot has to stay planted. Practice pivoting in both directions so you can protect the ball and find a pass or shot under pressure.

Get low on defense

A wide, bent-knee stance keeps you quick in both directions. Watch your opponent's chest, not the ball — it doesn't fake as easily as the eyes or shoulders do.

Build a free-throw routine

Same number of dribbles, same breath, same release, every single time. Consistency under no pressure is what makes the routine hold up under real pressure.

Condition, then play

Games are won in the fourth quarter by whoever's legs are still fresh. Add short sprint intervals and footwork drills to your practice, not just shooting reps.

Watch and study film

Watch how top players move without the ball, set up their defenders, and read passing lanes. Footwork and spacing translate to any level, even if the athleticism doesn't.

Play pickup games often

Drills build skill, but only real games teach decision-making under pressure. Get into as many live games as you can — that's where the reps actually stick.

Fast facts about the game

A handful of numbers and stories behind how basketball became what it is today.

1891

Dr. James Naismith invented basketball by nailing a peach basket to a gym balcony, needing an indoor game to keep his students active in winter.

13

The original game was played with just 13 rules and two peach-basket hoops — someone had to fetch a ladder to retrieve the ball after every score.

10ft

The rim has sat exactly ten feet off the floor since that very first game, unchanged even as players have grown taller and more athletic.

24s

The shot clock arrived in 1954, forcing teams to shoot within 24 seconds — a fix credited with saving the pro game from stalling out.

100

Wilt Chamberlain scored 100 points in a single game in 1962, a single-game scoring record that still stands more than sixty years later.

1946

The league that became the NBA was founded as the Basketball Association of America, merging with a rival league in 1949 to take its current name.

1979

The three-point line wasn't added to the NBA until the 1979–80 season, after years of use in rival leagues first proved it out.

5

Five players per team take the court at once — a number carried over from Naismith's original rules and never changed since.
